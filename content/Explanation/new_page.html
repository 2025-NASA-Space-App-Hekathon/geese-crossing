  • Hessian Roughness (R₂)

    Derived from the curvature of the DEM using a Hessian-based operator across spatial scales.

    Large R₂ values and a heavy right-tailed histogram indicate sharp ridges, steep valleys, or strong curvature.

    Broad distributions or multiple peaks suggest a mixture of landforms (e.g., steep scarps and flat plateaus).

  • Cosine Roughness (R₁)

    Computed from the dot product between the surface normal and the vertical axis (z-direction).

    Higher values correspond to flatter terrain, while lower values indicate steeper slopes.

    A left-skewed histogram therefore reflects a landscape dominated by steep, rugged surfaces.

  • Glacial and periglacial mountains (Himalaya, Alps, Japanese Alps) exhibit large R₂ tails and low R₁ means—typical of active frost weathering and strong slope processes.
  • Plateau–scarp systems (Andes, Tien Shan) show bimodal curvature distributions due to the juxtaposition of flat uplands and steep flanks.
  • Old or peneplained mountains (Great Dividing Range, Atlas, Taebaek) display narrow, high-R₁ histograms, indicating smooth, uniform surfaces with minimal structural relief.

3. Radar Vegetation Index (RVI)

The Radar Vegetation Index (RVI) provides a quantitative measure of surface characteristics derived from polarimetric SAR data.

It effectively distinguishes between bare ground, snow-covered terrain, and vegetated areas, reflecting differences in surface roughness, moisture, and canopy structure.

General Interpretation

  • RVI ≈ 0 → Predominantly dry or rocky terrains
  • RVI ≈ 1 → Predominantly moist or densely vegetated regions

1. Snow-Covered Areas

  • Shallow snow or icy surfaces → RVI low
  • Deep snow, wet snow, or multiple scattering conditions → RVI medium to high

2. Vegetated Areas

  • Deciduous / coniferous forests, shrublands → RVI high
  • Grasslands or sparsely vegetated regions → RVI medium to high

3. Bare Rock / Dry Terrain

  • Arid highlands, exposed rocky areas, smooth plains (low surface roughness) → RVI low

4. Cross Polarization Ratio

The VH/VV ratio serves as a complementary indicator of vegetation density and surface moisture, reflecting the same physical process as the Radar Vegetation Index (RVI) — the balance between volume and surface scattering.

  • High VH/VV → strong depolarization → dense canopy, moist vegetation, or snow
  • Low VH/VV → dominant surface reflection → bare or rocky terrain

Histograms of VH/VV and RVI show similar global patterns, confirming their shared sensitivity to vegetation structure.

However, minor deviations arise, and we expect that this is because of:

  1. Topography and slope (affecting VV more strongly),
  1. Snow wetness or mixed canopy (altering multiple scattering),
  1. Nonlinear weighting in RVI (4σVH term vs. simple ratio).

In summary, RVI and VH/VV together offer a robust interpretation of mountain surface characteristics, capturing both moisture variability and structural heterogeneity.

Contemporary Interpretation

SARchive: A Journey to See Earth More Clearly through Radar Data

Our goal was simple: to turn radar data into understanding — and that understanding into something we can share. What began as a set of small questions about mountain systems has evolved into SARchive, a platform that connects data, geography, and imagination to help us see our planet more distinctly.

The Power of SAR Data

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) data, when combined with Digital Elevation Models (DEMs), allows for quantitative analysis of mountain morphology and environmental conditions.

Quantitative Surface Interpretation

Polarimetric indicators such as the Radar Vegetation Index (RVI) and cross-polarization ratio (VH/VV) reveal variations in surface roughness, moisture, and canopy structure. These metrics distinguish bare ground, snow-covered regions, and vegetated zones by capturing the balance between volume scattering and surface scattering, highlighting both hydrological changes and structural heterogeneity.

Morphological Complexity

Through Hessian roughness (R₂) and Cosine roughness (R₁), we explored the curvature and slope of terrain. Interestingly, these two metrics capture different aspects of landscape geometry — their mutual information is only 0.07 bits, about 0.5% of their individual entropy (~13 bits) when we calculated for global data. This indicates that these multiple, independent measures are necessary to fully describe the structural complexity of mountainous terrains.

Multi-Dimensional Environmental Analysis

By combining SAR-based indices (RVI, VH/VV) with ecological metrics such as the Forest Degradation Index (FDI), we can interpret not only elevation or precipitation patterns but also the ecological stability and climatic heterogeneity of mountain ecosystems.

Key Insights from Global Mountain Analysis

The integration of SAR indicators and geographical data reveals how each mountain system carries a distinct geomorphological and ecological identity.

Topographic Diversity

The Himalayas exhibit both the highest mean elevation and the greatest variance, encompassing everything from tropical foothills to 8,000-meter snow peaks. The Andes show extreme elevation contrasts — from the coastal lowlands to the Andean plateau — while the Taebaek and Great Dividing Range display smooth, eroded forms with low elevation variance, reflecting ancient, stable mountain systems.

Geomorphological Evolution

Roughness metrics provide a clear morphological classification.

Glacial and periglacial ranges such as the Himalayas, Alps, and Japanese Alps feature heavy right-tailed R₂ distributions and low mean R₁ values, indicating steep slopes shaped by frost weathering and active mass movements. Plateau–scarp systems like the Andes and Tien Shan exhibit bimodal curvature distributions, reflecting the juxtaposition of flat uplands and steep escarpments. In contrast, ancient ranges such as the Great Dividing Range, Atlas, and Taebaek show narrow, high-R₁ histograms, indicating smooth and uniform surfaces with minimal structural relief.

Climate and Vegetation Patterns

SAR indices highlight subtle climatic and ecological contrasts.

Moist temperate regions like the Taebaek, Japanese Alps, and Great Dividing Range show high mean RVI values with very low variability (σ ≤ 0.15), reflecting stable forest cover and consistent VH backscatter. Their low FDI values (≤ 0.65) indicate well-preserved forest landscapes.

Continental highlands such as the Rocky Mountains, Himalayas, and Tien Shan display high RVI variance, revealing strong spatial heterogeneity across deserts, grasslands, coniferous forests, and glaciers. A positive correlation between elevation and RVI variability suggests that diverse scattering mechanisms — from snow to vegetation to rock — coexist at higher altitudes.

Meanwhile, ranges such as the Atlas, Tien Shan, and Himalayas show elevated FDI means (≥ 0.72–0.77), signifying degraded or sparsely vegetated surfaces under arid or alpine conditions.

Precipitation and Terrain Complexity

Topographic complexity tends to increase the variance of precipitation within a single system. The Alps, Andes, and Himalayas demonstrate this vividly, encompassing multiple climatic zones within one range. In particular, the Himalayas show one of the world’s strongest precipitation gradients — with humid monsoonal slopes to the south and arid plateaus to the north. Conversely, ranges such as the Taebaek, Atlas, and Tien Shan maintain low mean precipitation and variance, indicating dry and relatively uniform hydroclimatic conditions.

Conclusion

The SARchive project demonstrates that radar data can reveal the unseen structure of Earth’s mountains — their curvature, texture, and environmental contrasts — with remarkable precision. By fusing SAR-derived roughness and vegetation indices with classical geomorphological data, we can better understand how climate, erosion, and structure interact across scales.

Ultimately, SARchive is more than a dataset; it is an evolving way of seeing — transforming the invisible patterns of radar reflections into a coherent story about our planet’s dynamic surface.
