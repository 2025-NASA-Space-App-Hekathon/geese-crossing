Mountains are among the most dynamic and complex landforms on Earth, shaped by a wide range of physical, geological, and climatic processes — including precipitation regimes, lithology, tectonic history, and erosional forces.

To understand these systems in a unified and quantitative framework, our study conducts a global comparative analysis of major mountain ranges using both physical and remote sensing perspectives.

By integrating Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) observations with high-resolution Digital Elevation Models (DEM), we extract a set of surface and environmental features that represent each mountain’s morphology and ecological condition.

SAR-based parameters, such as polarimetric backscatter ratios (VV, VH), Radar Vegetation Index (RVI) and Forest degradation Index, provide insights into vegetation density, soil moisture, and surface texture—even in areas obscured by clouds or snow.

Meanwhile, DEM-derived roughness metrics (Hessian and cosine-based analyses) quantify the topographic ruggedness and structural complexity of each range.

By combining these remote sensing features with known physical drivers (e.g., precipitation distribution, rock composition, and formation age),

our study aims to construct a comprehensive morphological and environmental portrait of the world’s mountains.

This integrated framework bridges geological context and satellite-observed surface properties,

offering a new quantitative approach to explore how Earth’s mountain systems differ, evolve, and interact with climate and vegetation across scales.

I. Why Synthetic Aperture Radar , SAR?

1. Definition and Principle of SAR

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is an active remote sensing system.

SAR emits microwave pulses toward Earth’s surface and records the backscattered echoes.

Because microwaves are self-generated and longer in wavelength than visible light, SAR can acquire data day and night, through clouds, smoke, or even vegetation canopies and soil layers, depending on the wavelength.

This enables continuous monitoring in regions where optical sensors fail — such as tropical rainforests, mountain ranges, and polar zones.

2. Why “Synthetic Aperture”?

Achieving fine radar resolution is constrained by three interdependent physical factors:

  1. Aperture size (D): the physical length of the antenna.
  1. Wavelength (λ): the energy scale of transmitted microwaves.
  1. Feasibility of operation: the practical limitation of building and flying extremely long antennas.

The angular resolution θ of a radar system follows the relationship:

θ∼λ/D

This means that sharper (smaller) angular resolution requires either a shorter wavelength or a larger antenna.

However, both cannot be pushed arbitrarily because there is tradeoff: short wavelengths are heavily scattered by the atmosphere and surface roughness, while extremely large antennas are mechanically impossible to deploy on aircraft or satellites.

For example, at a wavelength of 5 cm, achieving a 10-meter spatial resolution would theoretically require a 4-kilometer-long antenna — a clear violation of practical engineering constraints.

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) resolves this paradox by synthesizing a large aperture virtually.

Instead of constructing a 4-km physical antenna, the radar moves along its flight path and records coherent backscatter echoes over time.

By combining these signals coherently, SAR reconstructs the same phase information that would have been captured by a giant stationary antenna.

Thus, the term “Synthetic Aperture” represents a logical and physical synthesis:

  • Using motion in place of structure,
  • Using coherence in place of size,

    to achieve high-resolution imaging without violating feasibility constraints.

3. Imaging Geometry and Distortion Mechanisms

Along-TrackDirection parallel to flight pathTime-domain synthesis

However, due to the nonlinear mapping between slant and ground ranges, mountainous terrain introduces distortions:

  • Layover: when the top of a mountain returns the signal before its base.
  • Shadow: when radar illumination fails to reach a slope facing away from the sensor.

These effects create a one-to-many mapping between pixel intensity and real-world surface geometry, which must be corrected during geocoding.

5. Polarization and Scattering Mechanisms

Each radar pulse can be polarized horizontally (H) or vertically (V).

Different combinations reveal distinct surface scattering mechanisms:

Polarization ModeDominant MechanismSurface Type Example
VVMirror-like reflectionSmooth bare soil, calm water
VH or HVVolume scatteringLeaves, branches, vegetation
HHDouble-bounce scatteringTree trunks, buildings, man-made targets

Surface roughness ↑ ⇒ depolarization ↑

Hence, multi-polarization analysis forms the logical space where physical structure ↔ electromagnetic response are inferred.

6. Speckle Phenomenon

Observation: SAR images contain granular “noise.”

  • Let S₁, S₂, …, Sₙ = scatterers within a pixel.
  • Their phase differences cause constructive or destructive interference.

⇒ The resulting intensity pattern (speckle) is not random noise but a deterministic interference phenomenon.

To reduce speckle, analysts employ:

  • Multi-look averaging (spatial coherence reduction)
  • Temporal averaging (multi-date coherence)

7. Resolution vs Pixel Size

Resolution (R): minimum physical separation the sensor can distinguish.

Pixel Size (p): grid spacing during image resampling.

Although often conflated, R ≠ p.

If p < R, oversampling occurs (no new detail).

If p > R, information loss occurs.

Hence, the logical constraint:

Optimal Imaging Condition: p≈R\text{Optimal Imaging Condition: } p \approx R

Optimal Imaging Condition: p≈R

8. Interferometric SAR (InSAR)

When two SAR images (I₁, I₂) are acquired over the same area, their phase difference (Δϕ) encodes path length change Δr:

Δr=λΔϕ4πΔr=\frac {λΔϕ}{4\pi}Δr=4πλΔϕ​﻿

Thus, InSAR enables centimeter-level detection of ground deformation — from earthquakes, landslides, glacier movement, to urban subsidence.

Workflow:

Raw Signal → Calibration → Co-Registration → Interferogram Formation → Phase Unwrapping → Geocoding → Deformation Analysis

II. Dataset

2.1 Sentinel-1 SAR Dataset (GRD Level)

Dataset Overview

The Sentinel-1 constellation, operated by the European Space Agency (ESA) under the Copernicus Programme, provides open-access radar data from C-band Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) sensors.

The mission consists of twin satellites — Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B (with Sentinel-1C to follow) — designed for continuous, all-weather Earth observation.

In this project, we used Ground Range Detected (GRD) products, which are radiometrically calibrated and orthorectified (geo-corrected) datasets.

These products represent radar intensity projected on the ground range coordinate system, suitable for direct analysis and visualization.

Acquisition Parameters in our Project

ParameterDescription
Instrument ModeIW (Interferometric Wide Swath)
Frequency BandC-band (5.405 GHz)
PolarizationsVV and VH (dual-polarization)
Processing LevelGRD (Ground Range Detected)
Data SourceGoogle Earth Engine (GEE)
Temporal CoverageFrom 2014
Access PolicyFree and open under Copernicus Data Policy

The VV and VH channels represent vertical–vertical and vertical–horizontal polarization components, respectively — corresponding to the radar’s transmitted and received electromagnetic orientations.

Their ratio provides a proxy for surface roughness and vegetation volume scattering.

Code Example: Sentinel-1 Data Import and Ratio Computation

def get_s1_layers(roi, start="2023-01-01", end="2023-12-31"):
    s1 = (
        ee.ImageCollection("COPERNICUS/S1_GRD")
        .filterBounds(roi)
        .filterDate(start, end)
        .filter(ee.Filter.eq("instrumentMode", "IW"))
        .filter(ee.Filter.listContains("transmitterReceiverPolarisation", "VV"))
        .filter(ee.Filter.listContains("transmitterReceiverPolarisation", "VH"))
    )
    img = s1.median().clip(roi)
    vv = img.select("VV")
    vh = img.select("VH")
    ratio = vh.divide(vv).log10().multiply(10).rename("VH_VV_ratio")
    return vv, vh, ratio

This script retrieves Sentinel-1 C-band dual-polarized images over a specified Region of Interest (ROI) and computes the logarithmic ratio (in dB) between VH and VV channels — a key metric for vegetation analysis.

2.2 Digital Elevation Model (DEM) Dataset

Dataset Overview

The Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data were obtained from the NASA Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM), which used both C-band and X-band radar sensors mounted on the Space Shuttle Endeavour in February 2000.

SRTM data provide a global elevation map covering latitudes between 60° N and 56° S, representing approximately 80% of Earth’s land surface.

Technical Specifications

ParameterDescription
Resolution30 arc-seconds (~1 km)
Vertical ReferenceWGS84 ellipsoid with EGM96 geoid correction
Acquisition MethodInterferometric single-pass (dual-antenna geometry)
CoverageGlobal (excluding high polar regions)
Data ProvidersNASA, NGA, DLR, and ASI (Italy)
AccessOpen data via NASA Earthdata Search

The SRTM mission derived elevation through Interferometric SAR (InSAR) techniques, processing simultaneous radar observations from two antennas to calculate terrain height.

Subsequent post-processing included void-filling using auxiliary datasets such as ASTER and GMTED, ensuring global coverage with minimal missing data.

Applications in This Study

The DEM dataset was used to:

  • Visualize Earth’s 3D topographic structure on a global sphere, and
  • Quantify the ruggedness and relief of each studied mountain range.

Combining DEM with Sentinel-1 SAR imagery enabled the correction of layover and shadow effects and improved understanding of how surface slope and roughness influence radar backscatter intensity.

III. Method

Here is the method of our analysis.

3.1 Overview of sentinel-1 Dataset

From the Sentinel-1 GRD level dataset, we extracted C-band backscatter intensity for VV (vertical–vertical) and VH (vertical–horizontal) polarizations.

By combining these radar measurements with topographic and mountain-range location data, we obtained physically interpretable features representing the environmental characteristics of mountainous regions.

Mountain environments exhibit complex interactions among topographic relief, bedrock exposure, vegetation cover, soil moisture, and snow conditions.

Therefore, the SAR backscatter intensity (σ⁰) observed in VV and VH channels reflects not only surface roughness but also the integrated structural and environmental state of the terrain.

3.2 Physical Interpretation of Polarization Signals

Sentinel-1’s VH polarization is sensitive to volume scattering within vegetation and moisture content, while VV polarization primarily responds to surface scattering and is strongly influenced by soil moisture and surface roughness.

PolarizationDominant Scattering MechanismHigh Backscatter (in Mountain Terrain)Low Backscatter (in Mountain Terrain)
VV (Vertical–Vertical)Surface scattering• Exposed rock and rough surfaces• Slopes facing the radar (specular reflection)• Moist or snow-covered surfaces with high dielectric constant• Dense forest canopy absorbing surface reflection• Radar shadow or steep shaded slopes
VH (Vertical–Horizontal)Volume scattering• Dense coniferous or broadleaf forests• Multiple scattering from branches, leaves, and trunks• Snow-covered or moist soils enhancing scattering• Bare or rocky surfaces with little vegetation• Thin snow or dry, smooth ground

Thus, VV generally represents surface reflectivity, whereas VH captures vegetation structure and water content within the mountain ecosystem.

3.3 Derived Vegetation and Surface Indices

We computed additional vegetation-related features from Sentinel-1 C-band backscatter:

  • Polarization Ratio:VH/VV Ratio=10log10(σVVσVH)10log_{10}(\frac{σ_{VV}}{σ_{VH}})10log10​(σVH​σVV​​)﻿

    → Relative measure of volume vs. surface scattering

  • Radar Vegetation Index (RVI)

    RVI=4σVHσVH+σVVRVI = \frac{4\sigma_{VH}}{\sigma_{VH} + \sigma_{VV}}RVI=σVH​+σVV​4σVH​​﻿

    → Empirical indicator of vegetation density and moisture

All ratio-based quantities were normalized within the Region of Interest (ROI) to ensure comparability between mountain systems.

3.4 Interpretation of VH/VV Ratio (in dB Scale)

VH/VV Ratio Range (dB)InterpretationEnvironmental Condition
High Strong VH response relative to VVVegetation-rich, snow-covered, or moisture-abundant zones
LowWeak VH relative to VVDry, rocky, or sparsely vegetated alpine areas

In general, higher VH/VV ratios indicate enhanced volume scattering (forested or wet conditions),

while lower ratios indicate dominant surface reflection (exposed rock, arid slopes, or glacially polished terrain).

3.5 Roughness 1 & Roughness 2

The surface roughness represents the morphological complexity and structural variability of mountain terrains.

In this study, two complementary roughness indicators were derived from the Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data — one based on the first-order derivative (slope gradient) and the other on the second-order derivative (surface curvature).

(1) Roughness 1 — Normal Vector-Based First-Order Roughness

Roughness 1 quantifies the degree of surface slope and directional variability using the local surface normal vectors.

For each DEM pixel, the normal vector is computed and its inner product with the vertical z-axis vector is used to obtain the angle (θ) between them.

This procedure is extended to the four surrounding triangular planes, and the average of their cosine values is calculated.

A value close to 4 indicates a flat terrain, whereas values approaching 0 represent steep and highly inclined surfaces.

Thus, Roughness 1 serves as a first-order derivative metric that characterizes the overall slope intensity and anisotropy of the terrain.

(2) Roughness 2 — Hessian-Based Second-Order Roughness

Roughness 2 captures finer-scale morphological variations, including convexity and concavity, which cannot be fully represented by slope-based metrics.

A Gaussian-scale Hessian analysis was applied to the DEM to compute the second derivatives of elevation over a fixed spatial window (e.g., 5 km).

From the resulting Hessian matrix, the principal curvatures (λ₁, λ₂) and their orientations were extracted, enabling the estimation of ridge and valley strength at each location.

This approach effectively quantifies the local curvature and morphological patterns of the terrain, providing a multi-scale view of its structural complexity.

(3) Morphological Significance

Together, these two roughness metrics provide a complementary description of terrain morphology:

  • Roughness 1 reflects the macro-scale slope and orientation of the surface (first-order variation),
  • Roughness 2 emphasizes micro-scale curvature and form (second-order variation).

By integrating both indicators, the study quantitatively captures the morphological heterogeneity of mountain systems,

offering a robust representation of the terrain morphology as a key feature in subsequent geospatial and environmental analyses.

Cross polarization rario is another indicator closely related to the spatial distribution of vegetation and surface moisture.

It can be interpreted in the same physical context as the Radar Vegetation Index (RVI), since both express the relative contribution of volume scattering (VH) to surface scattering (VV) within the observed area.

  • High VH/VV ratio → strong depolarization and volume scattering → dense canopy, snow, or moist vegetation zones
  • Low VH/VV ratio → dominant surface reflection → bare, dry, or rocky terrain

When histograms of VH/VV ratio are compared with those of RVI across multiple mountain ranges,

the overall pattern shows strong similarity, confirming that both indices capture the same physical process — the ratio between surface and volumetric scattering.

However, minor differences in histogram shape appear among certain ranges.

These differences are likely due to empirical and structural factors such as:

  1. Local topography and slope orientation — radar incidence angle affects VV response more strongly than VH, shifting the ratio distribution.
  1. Snow wetness or canopy structure — when the scattering medium changes (e.g., wet snow or mixed vegetation), RVI remains high but VH/VV ratio may slightly decrease due to multiple reflections.
  1. Calibration and normalization differences — RVI uses a nonlinear weighting (4σVH term), whereas VH/VV is a simple ratio, making it more sensitive to low backscatter values in dry or shaded areas.

Thus, although the cross-polarization ratio and RVI provide nearly equivalent vegetation sensitivity,

their histogram shapes may exhibit subtle deviations depending on terrain slope, snow conditions, and local surface roughness.

These nuances highlight the importance of using both indices together to robustly interpret mountain surface and vegetation structure.
